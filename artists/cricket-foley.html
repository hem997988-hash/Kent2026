Cricket Foley

Untitled

2D, CERAMICS · 2026

Over time, I have grown as an artist by pushing myself beyond what feels comfortable and familiar. Throughout my work, I challenged myself to take risks, whether that meant working on a larger scale, experimenting with new materials, or letting go of techniques I usually rely on. This process has shown me that I am capable of more than I thought, as long as I'm patient and willing to learn.


My large-scale floral pen drawing represents a big turning point for me. Before this piece, I had never worked on such a large scale. A photograph by Jochen Lempert inspired me to include bees, emphasizing the quiet beauty of nature. The hummingbird holds personal meaning, connecting to my grandfather, who loved birds. Through this, I hope my audience feels a sense of memory and connection with nature.


My Rhino and Fish abstract realism drawings show my origin point within my art. My comfort and true passion for art has always stemmed from penmanship; it's my most comfortable form. Drawing animals allowed me to explore different textures and patterns, while also portraying emotion.


My watercolor paintings pushed me differently. I had to step away from pen and fully commit to watercolor techniques. At first, I struggled and even restarted, but this taught me patience. Inspired by Monet, I explored light, softness, and color through the idea of looking both inside and outside a window. These compositions play with perspective and the boundary between interior life and the natural world.


In my ceramic breakfast set, I continued my floral imagery through underglaze pencils. Drawing on three-dimensional surfaces was unusual for me, as I had to consider how designs wrap around curved forms and are viewed from different angles. This challenged my sense of composition and made me more intentional. I created a cohesive set meant for everyday use, bringing art into daily routines. I researched historical traditions of decorated ceramics, inspiring my set, such as Majolica ware, Delftware, and East Asian underglaze painting. Understanding how artists have historically designed ceramics, compared to the present (primarily with flowers), to convey storytelling and pattern.


As I began Advanced Studies, I was more hesitant and relied on what I already knew. Throughout this journey, my work has become more experimental and ambitious. I now take more risks and spend more time thinking through my ideas, expressing patience within nature.
